Responsive Grid

A responsive grid takes it a step further.

Responsive Grid

Now, for this demo I'm just using the basic outline of a grid. These boxes could contain any kind of content — text, images, buttons, and so on.

These are two examples that take different approaches to responsiveness.

At the top are three sections laid side-by-side and split into equal thirds.

Down here, in red, we have a similar side-by-side layout. But lets assume that the content in these red areas can't get much narrower and still be usable.

Let's see how these two arrangements react when we go down to the size of a tablet.

click tablet

Your eye was probably drawn to the red area where the pieces are now stacked vertically. This ensures each one has enough width for its content.

But up here, the blue layout is no longer split into thirds. mouse around right side Because the content in this piece needs more width, it has actually expanded to take up half of the screen. Over on the left the pieces have been shrunk down to compensate.

Let's take grids a step further.

click 'Advanced' button

Now we have two separate structures nested together. At this size, most of the pieces are stacked on top of each other and they're pretty symmetrical.

Let's go back to a wider screen. click desktop

Now everything is side-by-side, all in one row. The pieces are using different proportions then before — they're no longer symmetrical. This is all the same HTML and the same content, it's just laid out differently to maintain legibility.

The code behind this is not complex. It's the approach to your page's structure that requires some thought. You really have to take a moment to look at your content on different-sized screens to get a feel for what works best.
